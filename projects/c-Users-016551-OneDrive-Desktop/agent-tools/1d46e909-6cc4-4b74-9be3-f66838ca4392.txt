Hasselblad has just made its most substantial upgrade to their mirrorless medium format cameras yet with the new X2D 100C. Boasting 100 megapixels, an internal terabyte of storage and very effective IBIS, it looks great on paper. To find out how it does in the field, Jordan went out with professional photographer[Ryan HK](http://www.ryanhk.com)to out where this camera delights, and where it irritates.

@Notwitholding: "It’s a shame that there isn’t a focal plane shutter, since it precludes any cheap/manual 3rd party lenses"
And some think "it’s a shame" Fuji doesn't offer native leaf shutter lenses and that the adapter they offer for Hasselblad H-series lenses is manual focus only unlike the Leica or X-system adapters for using H lenses.
Hasselblads are leaf shutter cameras by design. Adding a focal-plane shutter would by necessity increase the size and weight of the camera. They are designed for photographers for whom leaf shutter is a priority. If a focal-plane shutter for "cheap/manual 3rd party lenses" is a priority you can buy a different system, there are many available.
The X2D can adapt H lenses and accessories like their HTS 1.5 Tilt/Shift Adapter. Other lenses use the electronic shutter which has slow readout and limits, not "precludes", their use to appropriate subjects.
Having different systems designed for different individuals is a good thing.

The only side by side comparisons that I've seen of Fuji GFX 100S and Hasselblad X2D AF, so far, are from Matt Granger. Under most circumstances, either cameras's AF works well. The Fuji has the advantage of face detect, which helps in some situations. The ongoing issue that Granger has with the Fuji is that it detects faces in the background where there are none, mistaking some background pattern as a face and focusing there.
Anyone looking for the best AF performance as their top priority isn't going to find it in any medium format camera. They have never matched the features of smaller formats.
If you approach cameras as a sporting event looking for you team to win, the GFX 100S still wins the AF round. The X2D wins the IBIS, flash sync, and EVF rounds. If you're actually interested in creating images and how any cameras work for how you shoot and what you shoot, try them for yourself.
There is no camera that's best for everyone. That's why it's good to have distinctive choices.

Such backlit situations are notoriously difficult to reproduce because the sun can be many orders of magnitude brighter than a foreground subject, and exposure (and the degree to which the sun blows out) depends on the subject and time of day (and season, and latitude). I'm quite convinced that if you set your mind to it, you can make any AF system fail including SLR AF by choosing a comparatively very dark subject. I suppose clever cameras can stop down while focusing and find a sharp edge to black on the subject periphery. That's of course not the same as focusing on the internal structure of the subject, such as eyes, which some cameras (perhaps stupidly) will try to do.

[Richard Butler](https://www.dpreview.com/about/staff/richard)
A lower ISO (if there's an accordingly higher clipping point) will give better IQ by allowing the camera to accept more light, giving an SNR/tonal-quality improvement across the image.
Most image noise (especially anywhere other than the deep shadows) is essentially a representation of the randomness of light (photon shot noise). Consequently, the more light you sample, the less significant a role that noise plays.

Trying to entirely disentangle exposure and ISO is always good fun.
There's not an absolute connection between ISO and clipping point but typically most cameras' ISO settings will have a consistent relationship between ISO rating, exposure and clipping point.
ISO 64 can either be implemented as a expansion setting (essentially clipping earlier than the other settings, sometime thought of as an 'overexposed' version of base ISO), alternatively it can be a 'full' ISO setting with the same exposure-to-clipping relationship as the higher ISOs.
Which is why I included that caveat.
A lower 'full' base ISO will typically give better image quality, purely because it can be given more exposure.
