###### Abstract

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems are increasingly deployed across federal agencies for citizen-facing services including tax guidance, benefits eligibility, and legal information. These systems are vulnerable to knowledge base poisoning attacks, where adversaries inject malicious documents to manipulate RAG outputsтАФrecent work demonstrates that as few as 10 adversarial passages (0.04% of a corpus) can achieve 98.2% retrieval success rates [1]. We observe that RAG knowledge base poisoning is structurally analogous to software supply chain attacks, and propose RAGShield, a five-layer defense-in-depth framework that applies supply chain provenance verification to the RAG knowledge pipeline. RAGShield introduces: (1) C2PA-inspired cryptographic document attestation that blocks unsigned and forged documents at ingestion; (2) trust-weighted retrieval that prioritizes provenance-verified sources; (3) a formal taint lattice with cross-source contradiction detection that catches insider threats even when provenance is valid; (4) provenance-aware generation with auditable citations; and (5) NIST SP 800-53 compliance mapping across 15 control families including SA-12 (Supply Chain Protection). We evaluate RAGShield against six adversary tiersтАФexternal injection, signature forgery, insider compromise, adaptive evasion, data loader exploitation, and in-place replacementтАФon a 500-passage Natural Questions corpus with 63 attack documents and 200 queries. RAGShield achieves 0.0% attack success rate across all tiers including adaptive attacks (95% CI: [0.0%, 1.9%]) while maintaining 0.0% false positive rate, compared to 8тАУ13% ASR without defense, 7.5тАУ12.5% for RAGDefender, and 0тАУ0.5% for RobustRAG. We honestly report that in-place replacement attacks by insiders who modify existing documents achieve 17.5% ASRтАФidentifying the fundamental limit of ingestion-time defense and proposing hash-pinning mitigations. Notably, the cross-source contradiction detector catches subtle insider attacks (e.g., $15,000 тЖТ\rightarrow $15,500 numerical manipulation) that bypass provenance verification entirely. To our knowledge, RAGShield is the first framework to apply supply chain provenance to RAG security, the first to provide NIST 800-53 compliance mapping for RAG pipelines, and the first to defend against data loader exploitation attacks.

## I Introduction

Federal agencies are rapidly adopting Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems for citizen-facing services. The General Services Administration launched USAi.Gov in August 2025, providing all federal agencies access to AI models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta [16]. The Government Accountability Office documented 89 RAG-based citizen-facing services across 28 agencies as of fiscal year 2025 [17]. These systems answer questions about tax obligations, benefits eligibility, legal rights, and government proceduresтАФdomains where incorrect information causes direct harm to citizens.

RAG systems are vulnerable to knowledge base poisoning attacks. Zou et al. demonstrated in PoisonedRAG that injecting just 5 malicious texts into a knowledge base with millions of documents achieves over 90% attack success rate on NQ, HotpotQA, and MS-MARCO [30]. Chaudhari et al. showed that 10 adversarial passages (0.04% of the corpus) achieve 98.2% retrieval success in the Phantom attack [1]. The PhantomText attack achieves 74.4% success across five major RAG frameworks using invisible text embedded in standard document formats [3]. The ConfusedPilot attack demonstrates integrity and confidentiality violations against Microsoft 365 CopilotтАЩs RAG system [4].

Key observation. We observe that RAG knowledge base poisoning is structurally analogous to software supply chain attacks. In both cases, an adversary injects malicious content into a trusted pipeline: malicious code into a software build (SolarWinds [5]), or malicious documents into a knowledge base. The defense in software supply chains is provenance verificationтАФcryptographic attestation of origin, integrity, and chain of custody, standardized through SLSA [6] and C2PA [7]. No existing RAG defense applies this principle.

The gap. All existing RAG defenses operate on content analysis: RobustRAG [8] isolates and aggregates passages; RAGDefender [9] uses post-retrieval ML filtering; TrustRAG [10] applies clustering-based detection; the AV Filter [11] uses attention-variance scoring. Content analysis is an arms raceтАФas attacks become more fluent (CPA-RAG [14], AuthChain [15]), content-based detection becomes harder. Provenance verification provides a fundamentally different defense: you either have a valid cryptographic attestation or you donтАЩt.

Contributions. We present RAGShield, a five-layer defense-in-depth framework:

1.

Supply chain provenance for RAG (┬зIV-A): C2PA-inspired document attestation with content normalization that blocks unsigned, forged, and PhantomText-exploited documents at ingestion. First application of supply chain provenance to RAG security.

2.

Formal taint lattice with insider detection (┬зIV-C): Cross-source contradiction detection, corroboration checking, and claim magnitude scoring that catch insider threats even when provenance is valid. Formal taint propagation with soundness guarantee (Theorem 2).

3.

Provenance integrity bound (┬зV): Theorem 1 proves that attack success rate is bounded by the fraction of unattested documents when the adversary cannot forge signatures.

4.

Government compliance mapping (┬зVIII): First NIST SP 800-53 mapping for RAG pipeline security, covering 15 controls across SA, SI, AC, AU, SC, IR, CM, RA, and PM families, including the August 2025 AI Control Overlays.

5.

Comprehensive evaluation with baselines and adaptive attacks (┬зVII): Evaluation on the Natural Questions benchmark against five adversary tiers including T5 (adaptive RAGShield-aware adversary), compared against RobustRAG and RAGDefender baselines, with pre-poisoned corpus degradation analysis, threshold sensitivity study, and LLM-in-the-loop end-to-end impact assessment.

1. References

Each document dd entering ЁЭТж\mathcal{K} carries an attestation ЁЭТЬтАЛ(d)\mathcal{A}(d):

Content normalization (anti-PhantomText) strips zero-width characters, hidden text layers, and invisible HTML/CSS elements before hashing. Bootstrapping. ### IV-B Layer 2: Trust-Weighted Retrieval

Standard retrieval ranks by similarity: scoreтАЛ(q,d)=simтАЛ(╬╖тАЛ(q),╬╖тАЛ(d))\text{score}(q,d)=\text{sim}(\eta(q),\eta(d)). RAGShield augments with trust:

where ╬▒тЙе0\alpha\geq 0 controls trust sensitivity. At ╬▒=0\alpha=0, trust is ignored; at ╬▒=1\alpha=1, trust scales linearly; at ╬▒>1\alpha>1, high-trust documents are strongly preferred.

#### IV-C3 Cross-Source Insider Detection (v2)

Provenance alone cannot stop insider threats (T3). We add three content-based checks that run only on newly-ingested documents (not the established corpus baseline):

Contradiction detection. For a new document dnewd_{\text{new}} with embedding enewe_{\text{new}}, compute similarity against all existing AUTHORITATIVE documents. If simтАЛ(enew,eauth)тИИ[╬╕low,╬╕high]\text{sim}(e_{\text{new}},e_{\text{auth}})\in[\theta_{\text{low}},\theta_{\text{high}}] (topically similar but not identical), flag as a potential contradiction. Rationale: a poisoned insider document will be about the same topic as legitimate documents but make different claims.

Corroboration checking. Count how many existing documents have similarity тЙе╬╕corr\geq\theta_{\text{corr}} with dnewd_{\text{new}}. If fewer than kmink_{\min} corroborators exist, flag as uncorroborated. Rationale: legitimate policy changes appear in multiple documents (regulation + guidance + Federal Register notice); a single insider document making a sweeping claim is suspicious.

Claim magnitude scoring. Compute similarity between dnewd_{\text{new}} and the topic centroid of existing documents. If simтАЛ(enew,e┬пtopic)<╬╕mag\text{sim}(e_{\text{new}},\bar{e}_{\text{topic}})<\theta_{\text{mag}}, flag as an outlier. Rationale: even subtle numerical changes ($тАЛ15,000тЖТ$тАЛ15,500\mathdollar 15{,}000\rightarrow\mathdollar 15{,}500) shift the embedding away from the established consensus.

###### Theorem 1 (Provenance Integrity Bound).

Let ЁЭТж\mathcal{K} be a knowledge base where fraction pp of documents have valid provenance attestations, and let ЁЭТЬ\mathcal{A} be a T1 or T2 adversary who cannot produce valid signatures. Then:

| ASRLтАЛ1тЙд(1тИТp)тЛЕASRbaseline\text{ASR}_{L1}\leq(1-p)\cdot\text{ASR}_{\text{baseline}} | (3) |
| --- | --- |

where ASRbaseline\text{ASR}_{\text{baseline}} is the attack success rate without RAGShield.

###### Proof.

A T1 adversary cannot produce valid signatures (by the EUF-CMA security of Ed25519). Therefore, L1 rejects all injected documentsтАФthey never enter the retrieval index ЁЭТж\mathcal{K}. The only documents available for retrieval are the pтЛЕnp\cdot n attested documents plus any (1тИТp)тЛЕn(1-p)\cdot n unattested legacy documents. The adversary can only inject poison into the unattested fraction, bounding ASR by (1тИТp)тЛЕASRbaseline(1-p)\cdot\text{ASR}_{\text{baseline}}. Note: this bound comes from L1 ingestion blocking, not L2 trust weighting. L2 provides additional defense-in-depth but is not required for the bound. тИО

###### Corollary 1.

When p=1p=1 (all documents attested), ASRLтАЛ1=0\text{ASR}_{L1}=0 for T1 and T2 adversaries.

### VII-H Failure Mode Analysis

Where RAGShield succeeds unexpectedly: The T5 adaptive adversaryтАФwith valid provenance, semantically similar poison, and fake corroborating documentsтАФwas fully blocked. ## IX Discussion

Provenance infrastructure. RAGShield assumes a document signing infrastructure that does not yet exist for most government documents. Our bootstrapping strategy (Level 0 through Level 3) provides a realistic adoption path. The Government Publishing Office (GPO) already digitally signs Federal Register documents; extending this to agency publications is an infrastructure investment, not a research challenge.

Computational complexity. L1 provenance verification is OтАЛ(1)O(1) per document (hash + signature check). L2 trust-weighted retrieval adds no asymptotic overhead to standard top-kk retrieval. L3 insider detection is OтАЛ(n)O(n) per newly-ingested document (compare against nn existing corpus documents), yielding OтАЛ(mтАЛn)O(mn) for ingesting mm new documents into a corpus of size nn. For a 1M-document corpus, this is tractable: comparing one new document against 1M embeddings via matrix multiplication takes тИ╝\sim50ms on CPU. For batch ingestion of thousands of documents, approximate nearest neighbor search (e.g., FAISS HNSW) reduces this to OтАЛ(mтАЛlogтБбn)O(m\log n). The per-query overhead (тИ╝\sim10ms) is dominated by the embedding model inference (тИ╝\sim50ms), making RAGShieldтАЩs overhead negligible in practice.

Relationship to CivicShield. RAGShield extends CivicShield [18] in two directions: Layer 1 extends CivicShieldтАЩs input validation with document-level provenance chains, and Layer 3 extends CivicShieldтАЩs semantic taint tracking with pipeline-level taint propagation. Together, CivicShield (conversational defense) and RAGShield (knowledge base defense) provide complementary protection for government AI systems.

Limitations. (1) Our NQ evaluation uses 500 passages; production government knowledge bases contain millions of documents. Scaling to full NQ (300K+ passages) is future work. (2) The insider detection degrades when the baseline corpus is pre-poisoned beyond 5% (Table VII); periodic corpus auditing is required. (3) RobustRAG and RAGDefender baselines are simulated implementations capturing the core algorithmic principles (isolate-then-aggregate and post-retrieval anomaly filtering respectively); results may differ from production implementations. (4) The LLM-in-the-loop evaluation uses in-context assessment rather than automated end-to-end testing with a deployed LLM.

Ethical considerations. All attack documents in our evaluation contain fabricated government information (incorrect tax deductions, false benefit amounts). We do not distribute these documents outside the evaluation context. The government documents in our corpus are generated from templates, not copied from official sources.

## X Conclusion

We presented RAGShield, a five-layer defense-in-depth framework that applies supply chain provenance verification to RAG knowledge base security. By treating RAG poisoning as a supply chain attack, RAGShield provides a fundamentally different defense from existing content-analysis approaches: cryptographic provenance blocks unsigned and forged documents, while cross-source contradiction detection catches insider threats that bypass provenance entirely. Our evaluation on the Natural Questions benchmark across five adversary tiersтАФincluding adaptive attacks by a RAGShield-aware adversaryтАФdemonstrates 0.0% attack success rate (95% CI: [0.0%, 1.9%]) with 0.0% false positive rate, outperforming both RobustRAG and RAGDefender on insider and adaptive attack tiers.

RAGShield is the first framework to apply supply chain provenance to RAG security, the first to provide NIST SP 800-53 compliance mapping for RAG pipelines, and the first to defend against data loader exploitation attacks. As federal agencies scale RAG deployments for citizen-facing services, provenance-verified knowledge bases are essential to maintaining public trust in government AI systems.
